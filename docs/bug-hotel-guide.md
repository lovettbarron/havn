# Bug Hotel and Pollinator Water Stations Guide

**Project:** Haven -- A Learning Garden
**When:** Separate weekend activity, W17 or W18 (NOT on planting day)
**Duration:** ~30-45 minutes for bug hotel construction + 10 minutes for water station setup
**Who:** Father and child together

This is a standalone weekend activity, separate from the W16 planting day. It gives the child a second exciting project to look forward to after the big planting weekend and keeps garden engagement going across multiple weeks.

---

## Section 1: Nature Corner Placement

### Choosing the Spot

The bug hotel goes in a **shady, slightly damp spot away from the raised beds** -- creatures need their own undisturbed space, not right next to where you are digging, watering, and harvesting.

**Best location options** (see `docs/bed-layout.md` for the property layout):
- Against the fence or boundary on the **east or north side** of the backyard, near the mature trees
- These areas get afternoon shade from the tree canopy -- ideal for insects that prefer cool, damp conditions
- The nature corner should be at least 2-3 meters from the nearest raised bed

**Why away from the beds:**
- Log piles and stone piles attract woodlice, beetles, and earwigs -- some of which can nibble seedlings if they are too close
- Creatures need an undisturbed habitat; regular bed maintenance (watering, weeding, harvesting) would disturb them
- Having the nature corner in a different part of the garden creates a second destination for the child to visit ("first we check the beds, then we check the bug hotel")

### Mark the Spot on Planting Day (W16)

During the W16 planting day, walk to the chosen spot together and mark it:
- Place a stick or stone to mark the corner
- Tell him: **"This is where our bug hotel will go next weekend."**
- This builds anticipation for the next activity

## Section 2: Bug Hotel Construction (~30-45 min)

### Materials List

All materials can be collected from the garden and nearby nature areas. No purchases necessary.

| Material | Quantity | Where to Find | Purpose |
|----------|----------|---------------|---------|
| Logs (15-30 cm diameter sections) | 2-3 pieces | Garden, firewood pile, nearby woods | Base structure and beetle/woodlice habitat |
| Stones (various sizes, 5-20 cm) | Handful (8-12) | Garden, nearby paths | Gap filler, warm basking spots for insects |
| Pinecones | 10-15 | Under pine/spruce trees nearby | Lacewing and ladybird shelter |
| Hollow bamboo canes or plant stems | 10-20 pieces, varied diameters (5-15 mm) | Garden (old plant supports), craft store if needed | Solitary bee nesting tubes |
| Dead leaves | A few handfuls | Under trees, leaf litter | Ground beetle habitat, moisture retention |
| Bark pieces | 3-5 pieces | Fallen branches, firewood | Earwig shelter |
| Moss | Small clump | Shady spots in garden, north-facing walls | Moisture retention, aesthetic |
| **Optional:** 1-2 old wooden crates or a small pallet section | 1-2 | Genbrugsstation, storage shed | Frame for stacking materials vertically |

### The Treasure Hunt (~15 min)

Before building, send the child on a material collection mission. This is half the fun.

**Say:** "We need to collect building materials for the bug hotel. Can you find me pinecones, interesting stones, and sticks?"

Give him a bucket or bag and let him explore the garden and nearby area. He chooses what to collect. Father collects the heavy items (logs) and cuts bamboo to length (15-20 cm pieces) while the child is on his treasure hunt.

### Step-by-Step Build (~20-30 min)

#### Step 1: Clear the Area
- Clear a small patch (~60 x 60 cm) at the chosen nature corner spot
- Remove grass/weeds from the surface
- **He does:** Helps pull weeds and clear sticks from the area

#### Step 2: Base Layer -- Logs
- Stack 2-3 logs horizontally on the ground
- Position them with small gaps between them (beetles and woodlice live in these gaps)
- **Father does:** Heavy lifting and positioning of logs
- **He does:** Rolls smaller logs into position if he can

#### Step 3: First Fill -- Stones and Pinecones
- Wedge stones and pinecones into the gaps between the logs
- No glue or fasteners -- everything is loose-stacked
- **He does:** Chooses where each stone and pinecone goes
- Ask: **"Where should the pinecones go? Between the logs or on top?"**

#### Step 4: Bamboo Bundle -- Solitary Bee Tubes
- Bundle 10-20 hollow bamboo canes or stems together
- Wedge the bundle horizontally between the logs so the open ends face outward
- Solitary bees nest in these tubes -- they crawl in and plug the end with mud when they lay eggs
- **Father does:** Cuts bamboo to length (15-20 cm), ties the bundle loosely
- **He does:** Pushes the bundle into position between the logs

#### Step 5: Fill the Gaps
- Stuff dead leaves, bark pieces, and moss into all remaining spaces
- The more nooks and crannies, the better -- different creatures prefer different gap sizes
- **He does:** Stuffs leaves and bark into gaps (satisfying tactile activity)

#### Step 6: Optional Frame
- If you have old wooden crates or a pallet section, use them to create shelves
- Stack materials in layers inside the frame for a more vertical "hotel" structure
- This is optional -- a simple log-and-fill pile works perfectly well
- **Father does:** Structural assembly if using a frame

#### Step 7: Finishing Touches
- Sprinkle extra dead leaves around the base of the hotel (ground beetle habitat)
- Place a flat stone on top as a "roof" if you have one large enough
- **He does:** The final leaf scattering and any decorative touches he wants

### Naming the Bug Hotel

**Ask:** "What should we call our bug hotel?"

Let him name it. Write the name on a stone with a permanent marker, or he can paint a sign later as a mini craft project. Place the name stone in front of the hotel.

### What NOT to Do

| Do Not | Why |
|--------|-----|
| Use treated or painted wood | Chemicals deter insects and can leach into the soil |
| Use plastic materials | Plastic does not breathe, traps moisture, creates mold |
| Disturb the hotel after building | Creatures colonize over weeks -- moving or rearranging disrupts nesting |
| Expect instant results | First residents may take 2-4 weeks to move in; solitary bees are most active May-August |
| Place in full sun | Most hotel inhabitants prefer shade or partial shade; too much heat dries out the materials |

## Section 3: Pollinator Water Stations

Two water stations create two observation points for the child -- one at the nature corner and one near the flower-heavy beds.

### Station 1 -- At the Bug Hotel / Nature Corner

**Set up immediately after building the bug hotel.**

#### Materials
- 1 shallow dish (terracotta saucer, old plate, or similar -- 15-25 cm diameter)
- A handful of pebbles or small stones
- Clean water

#### Setup
1. Place the shallow dish on a flat spot next to the bug hotel
2. Fill the dish with pebbles and small stones until they poke above the water line
3. Add clean water to just below the top of the stones
4. Bees and butterflies land on the stones to drink safely -- they cannot swim, so the stones are landing platforms

**He does:** Places the pebbles in the dish and fills it with water from his watering can.

**Daily routine:** "First water the bug hotel dish, then water the beds." Build this into the daily watering routine so the dish stays full.

### Station 2 -- Near Flower-Heavy Beds

**Set up during W18 (or W17 if time allows).**

#### Materials
- Same as Station 1: shallow dish + pebbles + water

#### Setup
1. Choose a **sunny, sheltered spot** near the flower-heavy beds -- next to Bed B (His Bed) or between Bed A and Bed B
2. Position it where edible flowers (calendula, viola, borage) will attract pollinators once they bloom
3. Same construction: shallow dish, pebbles poking above water, water to just below stone tops

**He does:** Sets up the second station himself -- he knows the routine from Station 1.

**Say:** "Now the bees have two places to drink -- one at the bug hotel and one near your flowers. Check both when you water!"

### Water Station Maintenance

| Task | Frequency | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Refill with clean water | Every 2-3 days | Incorporate into the regular watering routine |
| Clean the dish | Weekly | Rinse out, replace water -- prevents mosquito larvae |
| Replace pebbles | Every few weeks (if needed) | Rinse off algae buildup |
| Winter | Remove or bring inside | Not needed when freezing; replace in spring |

**Mosquito prevention:** Mosquito larvae need 7-10 days of still water to develop. Cleaning the dish weekly breaks this cycle. If you forget for a week, dump and refill completely.

## Section 4: What to Watch For

A simple observation checklist the child can use after the bug hotel is built. Visit the nature corner together once a week and check each item.

### Creature Checklist

- [ ] **Woodlice** -- Look under the logs and in damp leaf litter (grey, many legs, curl into a ball)
- [ ] **Spiders** -- In the gaps between stones and in web corners of the structure
- [ ] **Earwigs** -- Under bark pieces and dead leaves (brown, pincer tail -- harmless)
- [ ] **Solitary bees** -- In the hollow bamboo stems. Look for plugged ends (sealed with mud = a bee laid eggs inside)
- [ ] **Beetles** -- Ground level around the base of the hotel, especially under leaves
- [ ] **Butterflies and bees at the water dishes** -- Watch from a short distance so you do not scare them
- [ ] **Ladybirds** -- On the logs and pinecones, especially in spring
- [ ] **Ants** -- Between stones and in soil gaps (they will definitely show up)

### How to Observe

- Visit the bug hotel together once a week
- Lift logs and bark gently to look underneath, then put them back carefully
- Do NOT remove creatures -- just look and count
- **"Draw or photograph any creature you find!"** -- He can keep a nature journal or use a phone camera
- Compare what you find each week: "Are there more woodlice this week than last week?"

### Seasonal Expectations

| Month | What to Expect |
|-------|---------------|
| April-May (just built) | Woodlice, ants, and earwigs first. They find the hotel within days. |
| May-June | Spiders build webs. Solitary bees start inspecting the bamboo tubes. |
| June-July | Solitary bee tubes may have mud plugs (eggs laid). Beetles active. |
| July-August | Peak activity. Butterflies and bees visit the water dishes regularly. |
| September-October | Activity slows. Some creatures prepare to overwinter in the hotel. |
| Winter | Leave the hotel undisturbed. Creatures are hibernating inside. |

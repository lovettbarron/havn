# Perennial Plant Care -- Overwintering and Year 2 Management

**Project:** Haven -- A Learning Garden
**Location:** Vejle, Denmark (USDA Zone 7b, maritime climate)
**Applies to:** All perennial plants across Bed A and Bed B

This guide covers how to prepare each perennial plant for winter, what to do in spring, and Year 2 management notes. All timing is specific to Zone 7b / Danish maritime climate conditions.

## Strawberry Crowns (Ostara + Korona)

**Location:** Bed B -- three strawberry plants: Ostara #1 at (25,35), Ostara #2 at (25,80), Korona #1 at (35,60)

### Autumn Care (Late October / November)

1. **After the last harvest** (September for Ostara everbearing, earlier for Korona June-bearing), trim dead and diseased leaves with scissors or by hand. Remove any brown, spotted, or wilted foliage.
2. **Do NOT mow or cut to the ground** in Year 1. The plants are still establishing their root systems. Leave the crown and any healthy green low growth intact.
3. **Remove runners** if any have developed. In Year 1, runners divert energy from crown establishment. Snip runners at the base.
4. **Wait for 2-3 hard frosts** before mulching (typically late October to mid-November in Vejle). The crowns need to harden off before being covered. Mulching too early traps heat and encourages soft growth that will be killed by later frosts.
5. **Apply 10-15cm straw mulch** over and around the crowns. Straw is ideal -- it insulates without matting down like leaves. Raised beds freeze faster than ground-level planting because the soil mass is smaller, so this mulch is important.

### Spring Care (March / April)

1. **Rake straw mulch aside** when new green growth appears at the crown centers. Do not wait too long -- the emerging leaves need light.
2. **Leave some straw** around the base of each plant as summer mulch. It suppresses weeds, retains moisture, and keeps fruit clean.
3. **Remove any dead leaves** that were hidden under the mulch.

### Year 2 Notes

- **Thin runners:** In the second year, the plants will produce more runners. Allow 2-3 runners per plant to root and fill gaps. Remove excess runners to keep plants at 15cm spacing minimum.
- **Remove weak plants:** If any crowns look small, poorly rooted, or unproductive, remove them and let a strong runner fill the space.
- **Renovation (after June harvest for Korona):** After the June-bearing Korona finishes fruiting, trim the oldest leaves to encourage fresh growth. Ostara (everbearing) keeps producing until September -- do not trim during the season.

## Raspberry Autumn Bliss

**Location:** Bed A south zone, 3 canes at center line W=60

### Understanding the Pruning Cycle

Autumn Bliss is a **primocane (autumn-fruiting)** variety. This means it fruits on canes that grew in the current year. The pruning cycle is different from summer-fruiting raspberries:

- Canes grow from the base in spring
- They flower and fruit in late summer / autumn (August-October)
- In February, ALL canes are cut to ground level
- New canes emerge from the roots in spring and the cycle repeats

**Do NOT prune Autumn Bliss in autumn.** The standing canes provide some frost protection to the root crown over winter.

### Autumn Care (W44-W46)

1. **Remove only dead or diseased canes.** If a cane is brown, snapped, or shows signs of disease (canker, discolored patches), cut it at ground level and dispose of it in household waste (not compost).
2. **Leave all other canes standing** through winter, even if they look untidy. They protect the root system.
3. **Apply a light mulch** (5cm straw or leaf mould) around the base of the canes to insulate the roots.

### February Pruning (Critical Timing)

1. **Cut ALL canes to ground level** (5-10cm stubs). Use sharp, clean secateurs. This is the single most important raspberry maintenance task of the year.
2. **Apply 5-8cm compost or well-rotted manure** as mulch around the base after cutting. This feeds the new canes that will emerge in spring.
3. **Do this in February** -- not earlier (canes still protecting roots), not later (new growth may have started).

### Year 2 Notes

- Year 1 (2026) is the **establishment year**. Expect a small harvest, possibly only a handful of berries. This is normal.
- Year 2 (2027) will see significantly more canes and a proper harvest from August onward.
- If canes are crowding (more than 8-10 per clump), thin to the strongest 6-8 canes in late spring.

## Thyme

**Location:** Bed A middle zone; Bed B edges -- creeping thyme at (5,5), (5,110), (5,145)

### Autumn Care

- **Do nothing.** Thyme is a hardy evergreen perennial that handles Zone 7b winters without intervention.
- **Do NOT prune in autumn.** Autumn pruning stimulates new soft growth that will be killed by frost.
- **Light mulch only in severe cold:** If temperatures drop below -10C for extended periods, add 2-3cm of straw around the base. The good drainage in raised beds helps thyme survive cold -- waterlogging is the bigger risk, not cold.

### Spring Care

- Thyme resumes growth naturally in spring. No action needed.
- **Light trim after flowering** (typically June/July) to keep the plants compact. Trim the spent flower stems and any leggy growth, but do not cut into old woody stems.

### Year 2 Notes

- Thyme becomes woodier over time. In Year 2-3, if plants look sparse in the center, take cuttings and propagate, or divide the plant.
- Creeping thyme varieties in Bed B will slowly spread along the edges -- this is desirable.

## Chives

**Location:** Bed A south zone

### Autumn Care

1. **Cut all foliage to 5cm** above soil level in late autumn (W44-W46). The leaves die back naturally, but cutting keeps the bed tidy.
2. No mulch needed -- chive bulbs are extremely hardy and survive temperatures down to -30C.

### Spring Care

- Chives are one of the first plants to show green growth in spring (March/April). No action needed -- just enjoy the early green shoots.

### Year 2 Notes

- Chives multiply rapidly. By Year 2, the clump may be large enough to divide. Dig up the clump in spring, split into 2-3 sections, and replant.
- If the plant flowers (purple pom-pom flowers in June), the flowers are edible and excellent for pollinators. Deadhead after flowering to prevent self-seeding if spread is unwanted.

## Lemon Balm

**Location:** Bed B, planted in a sunken pot at position (5,30)

### Autumn Care

1. **Cut stems to 15cm** above soil level. The top growth dies back in winter.
2. **Mulch the top of the pot** with 5cm of straw. The sunken pot provides good drainage, but the exposed pot rim means roots are more vulnerable to freezing than if planted directly in the bed.

### Spring Care

1. **Remove the straw mulch** when new growth appears at the base (March/April).
2. Lemon balm regrows vigorously from the root crown. Expect it to fill the pot quickly.

### Year 2 Notes

- The sunken pot contains lemon balm's spreading habit -- this is by design. If roots escape through the pot drainage holes, trim them.
- Lemon balm can become leggy. Harvest regularly (cut stems for tea or cooking) to keep the plant bushy.

## Mint

**Location:** Pot beside Bed B (contained to prevent spread)

### Autumn Care

1. **Cut all stems to ground level.** Mint dies back completely in winter and regrows from the roots in spring.
2. **Protect the pot from deep freezing.** Pots freeze faster than beds because the soil mass is smaller and all sides are exposed. Options:
   - Wrap the pot with bubble wrap (or hessian/burlap) for insulation
   - Move the pot against the house wall where it gets radiant heat
   - If the pot is small enough, move it into a cold but frost-free space (garage, shed) for the worst of winter
3. **Do NOT bring mint indoors** to a heated room -- it needs a cold dormancy period.

### Spring Care

1. **Remove pot wrapping** when risk of hard frost has passed (late March/April).
2. **Check soil level** -- pot soil compacts and may need topping up with fresh potting mix.
3. Mint regrows aggressively. New shoots appear from the roots once temperatures rise above 5C.

### Year 2 Notes

- If mint becomes rootbound (roots circling the pot, poor growth), repot into a slightly larger container with fresh potting mix in spring.
- Harvest often and cut flowering stems to keep the plant bushy and productive.

## Lamb's Ear

**Location:** Bed B, center of west edge at position (5,60)

### Autumn Care

1. **Remove dead or mushy leaves.** In autumn, some outer leaves will rot, especially if they have been in contact with wet soil. Pull off any brown, slimy, or damaged leaves by hand.
2. **Leave the rosettes intact.** The silvery-green leaf rosettes at the center of the plant are alive and will resume growth in spring.
3. **No special winter protection needed.** Lamb's ear is fully hardy in Zone 7b and tolerates cold and frost without issue. Its biggest enemy is winter wet, not cold -- the good drainage in the raised bed helps.

### Spring Care

- Remove any remaining dead outer leaves in early spring.
- New silvery leaves emerge from the center rosettes.
- No other action needed.

### Year 2 Notes

- Lamb's ear may spread modestly by Year 2. If it encroaches on neighboring plants, divide or trim the edges.
- If the plant sends up flower spikes (tall, fuzzy stems), these are not particularly ornamental. Cut them off to keep the plant compact, unless the child enjoys the texture (they are very fuzzy and tactile).

## Key Reminders

- **Raised beds freeze faster** than ground-level plantings. The smaller soil mass and exposed sides mean roots experience colder temperatures. Extra mulch matters.
- **Do not mulch perennials too early.** Wait for 2-3 hard frosts so the plants harden off first. Early mulching traps heat and encourages vulnerable soft growth.
- **Straw is the preferred mulch** for winter protection. It insulates without matting, stays airy, and is easy to rake aside in spring.
- **Never compost diseased plant material.** Bag it in household waste to prevent spreading disease to next year's garden.
- **February is raspberry month.** Set a calendar reminder for February to prune Autumn Bliss canes and apply compost mulch.

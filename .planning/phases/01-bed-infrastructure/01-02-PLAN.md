**File 1: `docs/soil-layers.md`**

Create a soil layering guide with the following structure:

1. **Overview** -- Three-layer system: drainage (bottom), garden waste (middle), hojbedsmuld (top). Explain the purpose of each layer in one sentence.

2. **40cm Backyard Beds (Beds 1-3)** -- Specific instructions:
   - Bottom 8-10cm: Drainage gravel (16-32mm singels). Spread evenly across the bed floor. Purpose: prevents waterlogging in Danish autumn rains.
   - Middle 10-15cm: Garden waste -- branches (broken to 10-20cm), fallen leaves, grass clippings, straw. Pack loosely. Purpose: slow-release nutrients as it decomposes (hugelkultur principle), reduces volume of expensive purchased soil.
   - Top 15-20cm: Hojbedsmuld (purchased raised bed soil mix). Level and lightly tamp. This is the growing medium.
   - Note for reservoir beds (Beds 1 and 2): The drainage layer is replaced by the reservoir system. See `reservoir-build.md` for the bottom ~10cm -- then garden waste and hojbedsmuld layers go on top as normal.

3. **30cm Terrace Beds (Beds 4-5)** -- Modified for lighter weight:
   - Bottom 5cm: Drainage gravel (use perlite or lightweight expanded clay aggregate / LECA as alternative to reduce weight)
   - Middle 8-10cm: Coconut coir or lightweight compost (avoid heavy garden waste -- weight matters on terrace)
   - Top 15cm: Hojbedsmuld
   - Note: Using LECA instead of gravel and coir instead of garden waste reduces saturated weight by approximately 20-30%.

4. **Volume Calculations** -- Table showing:
   | Bed | Dimensions | Total Volume | Drainage | Middle | Top Soil |
   For each of the 5 beds with volumes in liters.
   - Backyard bed: 120x60x40cm = 288 liters total
   - Terrace bed: 80x40x30cm = 96 liters total
   - Total hojbedsmuld needed: sum of all top layers (~0.5-0.6 m3)
   - Total gravel needed: sum of drainage layers for non-reservoir beds (~0.05 m3)

5. **Tips** -- Fill beds and water thoroughly. Soil will settle 5-10cm over the first few weeks. Top up with hojbedsmuld after settling. The child can help spread the top soil layer (manageable task).

**File 2: `docs/slug-defense.md`**

Create a copper tape application guide:

1. **Overview** -- Copper tape creates a mild galvanic reaction with slug slime, deterring crossing. Effectiveness: 60-85% with proper 50mm+ width application. Combined with 40cm bed height, this provides strong slug protection.

2. **Materials** -- 50mm-wide self-adhesive copper tape, rubbing alcohol, clean cloth, optionally small stainless steel screws or rivets for reinforcement.

3. **When to Apply** -- Apply on assembly day, before corten patina develops. New steel surface provides better adhesive bond. For galvanized beds, surface is already smooth -- clean and apply.

4. **Step-by-Step Application:**
   - Step 1: Clean the outside surface of the bed with rubbing alcohol, approximately 5cm below the top rim. Wipe dry.
   - Step 2: Starting at one corner, peel backing and apply copper tape in a continuous band around the entire bed perimeter.
   - Step 3: At corners, FOLD the tape around (do not cut and restart -- gaps are failure points for slugs).
   - Step 4: Overlap the start and end by at least 2cm.
   - Step 5: Press firmly along the entire length to ensure adhesion.
   - Step 6 (optional but recommended): Secure the tape every 30cm with a small stainless steel screw through the tape into the bed wall. This provides mechanical backup against Danish weather cycling.

5. **Maintenance** -- Wipe tape with a damp cloth every 4-6 weeks during growing season to prevent patina buildup that reduces effectiveness. Inspect after storms for peeling edges.

6. **Per-Bed Tape Length:**
   - Backyard beds: perimeter = 2*(120+60) = 360cm = 3.6m each. 3 beds = 10.8m.
   - Terrace beds: perimeter = 2*(80+40) = 240cm = 2.4m each. 2 beds = 4.8m.
   - Total: 15.6m + overlap allowance = ~17m minimum. Two 25m rolls provide ample supply with margin for mistakes.

**File 1: `docs/reservoir-build.md`**

Create a DIY wicking bed reservoir guide. This is the most complex construction task in Phase 1.

1. **Overview** -- A sub-irrigated planter (SIP) uses a PVC pipe reservoir at the bottom of the bed. Water is added via a fill tube and wicks upward through the soil via capillary action. Provides 2-4 days of water autonomy per fill. Installed in Bed 1 (tomato) and Bed 2 (cucumber) only.

2. **Materials Needed (per bed):**
   - 3m of 50mm PVC pipe
   - 2x 90-degree PVC elbows
   - 2x PVC end caps
   - 1x piece of pond liner/EPDM (0.5mm+ thick), sized: bed length + 30cm x bed width + 30cm (to fold up sides)
   - 1x piece of geotextile fabric, sized: bed length x bed width
   - Coarse gravel (16-32mm) to fill around pipes, approximately 30 liters per bed
   - 1x vertical fill tube: 50cm length of 50mm PVC pipe (extends above soil line)
   - 1x overflow fitting or drilled hole at 8cm height from bed floor
   - Drill with 10-12mm bit
   - PVC cement (optional -- dry-fit works for garden use)

3. **Build Sequence (per bed):**
   - Step 1: Line the bed interior with pond liner. Fold liner up the bed walls to at least 10cm above the bed floor. The liner creates the waterproof reservoir zone.
   - Step 2: Cut PVC pipes to bed length (~110cm, leaving room at ends). Drill 10-12mm holes every 10cm along the top half of each pipe. These distribute water into the gravel.
   - Step 3: Lay 2-3 PVC pipes parallel across the bed floor on top of the liner, spaced evenly. Cap both ends. Connect with elbows if using a continuous loop design, or leave as independent channels.
   - Step 4: Install the vertical fill tube. Connect a 50cm vertical PVC pipe to one end of the horizontal pipe system using an elbow. This tube extends above the final soil line -- it is where you pour water to fill the reservoir.
   - Step 5: Install the overflow. Drill or cut a hole through the bed wall AND liner at exactly 8cm above the bed floor. Insert a short PVC stub or bulkhead fitting. This prevents the reservoir from flooding the soil zone above. CRITICAL: the overflow MUST work -- test with water before adding soil.
   - Step 6: Fill around the PVC pipes with coarse gravel, level with the top of the pipes (~8cm deep). The gravel creates reservoir volume between the pipes.
   - Step 7: Lay geotextile fabric over the entire gravel layer. This prevents soil from washing down into the reservoir while allowing water to wick upward.
   - Step 8: Continue with normal soil layering on top (garden waste + hojbedsmuld, per soil-layers.md). The reservoir replaces the drainage layer.

4. **Testing the Reservoir:**
   - Pour water into the fill tube until water flows from the overflow hole. Note how much water this takes (approximately 15-20 liters).
   - Wait 30 minutes. Check that the gravel zone is saturated and no water is pooling above the geotextile.
   - If water pools on top of the geotextile, the fabric may be too dense -- replace with a more permeable grade.

5. **Usage in Season:**
   - Fill reservoir via the tube every 2-4 days (more often in hot weather, less in rain).
   - The soil surface may look dry while roots are well-watered from below -- this is normal and actually reduces slug and fungus problems.
   - Before vacation: fill reservoir completely and water from the top as well. This maximizes water reserves.

6. **Diagram:**
   Include an ASCII cross-section showing: liner, PVC pipes, gravel, geotextile, soil layers, fill tube, overflow hole.

**File 2: `docs/trellis-build.md`**

Create an A-frame cucumber trellis guide:

1. **Overview** -- A permanent A-frame trellis sits over Bed 2 (cucumber bed). Two angled panels meet at a ridge. Cucumbers climb the mesh and hang down from underneath -- perfect picking height for a 7-year-old.

2. **Design Specifications:**
   - Height at peak: 120cm (child-reachable)
   - Base footprint: 120cm long (matches bed length) x 70cm wide (slightly wider than the 60cm bed to allow panel angle)
   - Panel angle: approximately 60 degrees from horizontal
   - Frame material: pressure-treated timber 45x45mm (class 3+) OR galvanized steel angle 25x25mm
   - Climbing surface: galvanized wire mesh (10x10cm grid) or UV-resistant nylon netting

3. **Materials:**
   - 4x frame legs: 150cm long (timber 45x45mm or steel angle 25x25mm)
   - 2x ridge connector pieces: 120cm long (same material as legs)
   - 2x mesh panels: ~120x130cm each (wire mesh or netting)
   - Ridge hardware: 2x T-hinges or bolted bracket connection
   - Base anchoring: 4x ground stakes or L-brackets to secure legs to the bed sides (prevent wind toppling)
   - Galvanized screws/bolts, cable ties (for mesh attachment)

4. **Build Sequence:**
   - Step 1: Cut 4 legs to 150cm. These form the A-frame sides.
   - Step 2: Cut 2 ridge pieces to 120cm (bed length). These run along the top where the panels meet.
   - Step 3: Attach mesh/netting to each pair of legs + ridge piece, forming two rectangular panels.
   - Step 4: Connect the two panels at the ridge using hinges or bolts. If hinged, the frame can fold flat for winter storage (optional -- user decided permanent installation, but hinge option allows seasonal adjustment).
   - Step 5: Spread the legs to create the A-shape. The legs should rest on the ground on either side of the bed, not inside it.
   - Step 6: Anchor the legs to the bed walls or ground using L-brackets or stakes. The trellis must resist wind.
   - Step 7: Test stability with a firm push from each side.

5. **Diagram:**
   Include an ASCII front view and side view showing the A-frame dimensions, bed position underneath, and approximate panel angles.

6. **Child Interaction Note:**
   The A-frame design is specifically chosen because cucumbers hang DOWN through the mesh, putting them at the child's eye and hand level. Unlike a vertical trellis where fruit is at the top, the A-frame creates a "cucumber tunnel" effect that is engaging for a 7-year-old.

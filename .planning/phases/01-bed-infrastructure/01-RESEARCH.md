## Summary

Phase 1 delivers the physical garden infrastructure: five raised beds (three corten steel backyard beds at 120x60x40cm and two galvanized terrace beds at 80x40x30cm), filled with layered soil, equipped with slug defense, self-watering reservoirs in two beds, and an A-frame cucumber trellis. The primary Danish supplier for metal beds is byJEMA, who manufactures in Denmark with 1-3 day handling and 2-7 day transit. The budget will be tight -- beds alone consume 5,000-6,500 DKK before soil, copper tape, reservoir materials, or trellis. The hybrid corten/galvanized strategy helps, but total material cost will likely land at 8,000-10,000 DKK, significantly above the 2,000-5,000 DKK target. The shopping list must present both the preferred corten option and a budget-conscious all-galvanized alternative.

The property has the backyard extending east/northeast from the house. The west-central yard area gets the best sun exposure (8-10 hours in summer) since mature trees cluster on the east boundary. The outdoor water tap is in the backyard -- beds should cluster near it for easy hose/watering can access by the child. The roof terrace (~70 m2, above the carport on the west side) previously held two small beds and furniture, giving reasonable confidence in load capacity, though weight calculations must be documented.

**Primary recommendation:** Order byJEMA CUBY beds (corten 120x60x40cm for backyard, galvanized 80x40x30cm or custom for terrace), fill with a three-layer system (drainage gravel, garden waste, hojbedsmuld), and build DIY wicking reservoirs using PVC pipe and pond liner in the tomato and cucumber beds.

### Pattern 1: Three-Layer Soil Fill System
**What:** Fill beds from bottom to top with drainage material, bulk organic matter, and growing medium
**When to use:** All five beds

Layer depths for 40cm backyard beds:
- **Bottom 8-10cm:** Drainage layer -- coarse gravel (16-32mm singels) or broken tiles/stones
- **Middle 10-15cm:** Garden waste layer -- branches, leaves, grass clippings, straw (free from own garden; acts as slow-release nutrients as it decomposes, similar to hugelkultur principle)
- **Top 15-20cm:** Hojbedsmuld -- purchased raised bed soil mix (composted manure, compost, sand blend)

Layer depths for 30cm terrace beds:
- **Bottom 5cm:** Drainage gravel
- **Middle 8-10cm:** Light garden waste or coconut coir (lighter weight for terrace)
- **Top 15cm:** Hojbedsmuld

### Pattern 2: DIY Wicking Bed Reservoir
**What:** Sub-irrigated planter (SIP) using PVC pipe reservoir beneath the growing medium
**When to use:** Tomato bed and cucumber bed only (the two highest-water-demand crops)

Construction sequence:
1. Line the inside of the bed with pond liner / EPDM (up the sides to above reservoir height)
2. Place 50mm PVC pipes horizontally at the bottom, capped at ends, with 10-12mm holes drilled every 10cm
3. Install a vertical 50mm PVC fill tube extending above soil line (water inlet)
4. Install an overflow pipe/hole at the top of the reservoir zone (~8cm from bottom) to prevent waterlogging
5. Fill around PVC pipes with coarse gravel to create reservoir volume
6. Lay geotextile fabric over the gravel to separate it from soil
7. Fill remaining depth with soil layers as normal

The reservoir holds approximately 15-20 liters per bed, providing 2-4 days of water autonomy depending on temperature and plant size.

### Pattern 3: Copper Tape Slug Defense
**What:** Self-adhesive copper tape applied around the outer rim of each bed
**When to use:** All five beds

Application method:
1. Clean and dry the steel surface where tape will be applied
2. Apply 50mm-wide copper tape in a continuous band around the outside of the bed, approximately 5cm below the top rim
3. Ensure no gaps -- overlap ends by at least 2cm
4. For corners, fold the tape rather than cutting and restarting (gaps are failure points)
5. Periodically wipe with a damp cloth to prevent patina buildup that reduces effectiveness

**Key finding:** Copper tape effectiveness is 60-85% when properly applied with 50mm+ width. It is a deterrent, not a guarantee. Combined with the raised bed height (40cm), it provides strong but not absolute slug protection.

### Pattern 4: A-Frame Cucumber Trellis
**What:** Permanent A-frame trellis spanning the cucumber bed
**When to use:** Cucumber bed only (INFRA-06)

Design specifications:
- Two angled panels (~120cm tall at peak) meeting at a ridge
- Base footprint matches bed width (60cm) or slightly wider
- Panels made from galvanized steel angle iron or pressure-treated 45x45mm lumber
- Climbing surface: galvanized wire mesh (10x10cm grid) or UV-resistant nylon netting
- Panels hinged or bolted at the ridge for potential seasonal adjustment
- Child height consideration: peak at ~120cm allows a 7-year-old to reach hanging cucumbers from underneath

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- **Placing beds against fences or walls:** Blocks access, creates slug highways, reduces airflow. All beds must be freestanding with 60-80cm paths on all sides.
- **Skipping the drainage layer:** Without gravel at the bottom, beds become waterlogged in Danish autumn rains. Always include 8-10cm drainage.
- **Using untreated wood for the trellis:** Will rot within 2-3 seasons in Danish humidity. Use galvanized steel or pressure-treated (class 3+) wood only.
- **Filling beds entirely with purchased soil:** Extremely expensive. The garden waste middle layer saves 30-40% of soil cost while improving long-term soil biology.
- **Placing terrace beds in the center of unsupported spans:** Weight must be distributed over structural supports (walls, beams). Position along the railing/edge where the carport structure provides support below.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Budget Shock
**What goes wrong:** The 2,000-5,000 DKK budget target is insufficient for corten steel beds from byJEMA plus soil, materials, and accessories. Three corten beds alone are ~4,335 DKK.
**Why it happens:** Corten steel is a premium material. The budget was set before specific product research.
**How to avoid:** Present the shopping list with clear total costs for BOTH the corten hybrid option AND an all-galvanized budget option. Let the user make an informed choice. Also identify where savings are possible (e.g., free garden waste for the middle layer, sourcing gravel locally).
**Warning signs:** If the plan does not include a clear budget comparison, it will surprise the user at ordering time.

### Pitfall 2: Terrace Overloading
**What goes wrong:** Saturated soil in raised beds is extremely heavy. A 80x40x30cm bed filled with wet soil weighs approximately 70-100 kg.
**Why it happens:** People underestimate how much water soil absorbs. Saturated soil weighs ~1,700 kg/m3.
**How to avoid:** Calculate exact weight per terrace bed: 80x40x30cm = 0.096 m3. At 1,700 kg/m3 saturated = ~163 kg per bed maximum. But with drainage layer and lighter fill, realistic saturated weight is 100-130 kg. Document this in the setup instructions. Position beds over structural supports (carport walls/beams below). The previous owners' beds provide precedent, but this should be explicitly documented.
**Warning signs:** Beds placed in the middle of an unsupported span; no weight calculation in the documentation.

### Pitfall 3: Reservoir Leaking into Bed Structure
**What goes wrong:** The wicking reservoir liner leaks, and water pools at the bottom of the steel bed without proper drainage, causing root rot.
**Why it happens:** Pond liner gets punctured during installation, or is not properly sealed at joins.
**How to avoid:** Use a single piece of EPDM liner (not multiple pieces joined together). Test the liner with water before adding soil. Ensure the overflow pipe/hole is functional. The overflow is critical -- it prevents the reservoir from flooding the soil zone above.
**Warning signs:** No overflow mechanism designed into the reservoir; liner pieced together from scraps.

### Pitfall 4: Copper Tape Peeling in Danish Weather
**What goes wrong:** Self-adhesive copper tape detaches from corten/galvanized steel after rain and frost cycles.
**Why it happens:** The textured surface of weathered corten provides poor adhesive grip. Temperature cycling loosens adhesive.
**How to avoid:** Clean the application surface thoroughly with rubbing alcohol before applying. Apply tape in warm, dry conditions. Consider securing edges with small stainless steel screws or rivets as backup. For corten, apply before the patina fully develops (the new steel surface bonds better).
**Warning signs:** Tape applied to wet or dirty surfaces; no mechanical backup fastening.

### Pitfall 5: Undersized Trellis
**What goes wrong:** The A-frame trellis is too short or too narrow, and cucumber vines outgrow it by midsummer.
**Why it happens:** Cucumber vines can reach 2+ meters in a good Danish summer.
**How to avoid:** Build the trellis at least 120cm tall. Cucumber vines will climb to the top and then hang down (which is actually desirable for an A-frame -- hanging cucumbers are easy to pick). Width should span the full length of the bed (120cm).
**Warning signs:** Trellis shorter than 100cm; no climbing mesh/netting attached.

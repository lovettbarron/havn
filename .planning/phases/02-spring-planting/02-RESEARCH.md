# Phase 2: Spring Planting - Research

**Researched:** 2026-03-26
**Domain:** Cold-hardy crop planting, companion planting layout, sensory garden design, child engagement sessions, creature habitats
**Confidence:** HIGH

## Summary

Phase 2 transforms five empty raised beds (built in Phase 1) into a functioning spring garden. The core deliverables are: (1) a bed-by-bed planting grid map with exact positions and spacing in cm, (2) physical planting of all cold-hardy crops, edible flowers, sensory plants, and berry perennials, (3) a planting day session script for the child, (4) a bug hotel/nature corner, and (5) two pollinator water stations. The phase targets W16 (mid-April) for the main planting day, with follow-up sessions W17-W18.

Key research findings that shape the plan: strawberries and raspberries must NOT share a bed (disease risk from verticillium wilt); peas and spring onions should NOT be planted adjacent (alliums stunt pea growth); Kelvedon Wonder peas are dwarf (60cm) and work well on the A-frame trellis as a pre-crop before cucumbers in W21; lemon balm spreads aggressively and needs containment even within a raised bed. The companion planting layout must respect these constraints while achieving the user's goals of sensory edge planting, functional flower distribution, and a child-centric "his bed" that delivers quick visual rewards.

**Primary recommendation:** Build the grid maps bed-by-bed, starting with the three locked assignments (his bed, raspberry bed, shared beds), using the spacing data in this research. Write the planting day session script as a separate deliverable. Treat the bug hotel and water stations as a distinct weekend activity (W17-W18).

### Pattern 1: Grid Map Format
**What:** A precise, printable layout for each bed showing plant positions in a scaled grid
**When to use:** Every bed gets its own grid map -- this is the SCHED-01 deliverable

**Key principles:**
- Sensory plants (lamb's ear, thyme, lemon balm) always on edges, especially the side facing the walking path
- Strawberries get the center bulk of the bed for maximum sun
- Quick-harvest crops (pea shoots, radish) in designated sections that can be re-sown
- Exact positions in cm from a 0,0 corner reference point

**What:** A step-by-step plan for the big W16 planting day, with timing, breaks, and transition cues
**When to use:** One script for the main planting day, adaptable for W17-W18 follow-ups

```markdown
## Planting Day Script -- Weekend W16

### Prep (before the child wakes up)
- [ ] Lay out tools, seeds, seedlings by each bed
- [ ] Fill watering cans
- [ ] Set out snacks and water bottle at the garden table

- Duration: 20-30 min
- He does: Pushes big pea seeds into holes along the trellis, helps carry tools

### Wrap-up (5 min)
- Put tools away together
- Place his painted markers in his bed
- Take a photo together with the planted beds

Total time: ~75-100 min with break
```

### Pattern 3: Bug Hotel / Nature Corner
**What:** A simple structure from natural materials placed in a shady corner away from the beds
**When to use:** Separate weekend activity W17 or W18

Construction approach:
1. Choose a shady, slightly damp spot away from the beds (against a fence or under a tree)
2. Base: Stack 2-3 logs horizontally to create the foundation
3. Layer: Add stones, pinecones, and bundles of hollow bamboo/stems between the logs
4. Fill gaps with dead leaves, bark, and moss
5. Optional frame: Use 2 old wooden crates or a small pallet as structure to stack materials in
6. Leave undisturbed -- creatures will colonize over weeks

Child involvement:
- He collects materials from the garden and nearby (pinecones, sticks, stones)
- He chooses where to place each material ("Where should the pinecones go?")
- He names the hotel ("What should we call our bug hotel?")
- After building: Set up the first pollinator water dish next to it

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- **Planting strawberries and raspberries in the same bed:** Verticillium wilt and other fungal diseases transfer between these species. Always in separate beds, ideally not adjacent.
- **Planting peas next to spring onions/alliums:** Alliums chemically stunt legume growth. Keep these in different beds or at maximum distance within the same bed.
- **Planting lemon balm without containment:** Lemon balm spreads aggressively via runners. In a raised bed, dedicate a corner section and consider a physical barrier (e.g., a terracotta pot sunk into the soil) or plant on the very edge where it can be trimmed.
- **Overcrowding the child's bed:** Tempting to pack in many exciting plants, but overcrowding reduces success rate. Every plant failure is magnified for a child with ADHD. Leave breathing room.
- **Scheduling the planting day too long:** 2+ hours without breaks will exceed the child's attention window. The session script must have a clear break after 30-40 minutes and a total time under 100 minutes.
- **Planting everything on one day:** W16 is the main event but succession sowing (radish, lettuce) continues W17-W20. The grid maps must show which sections are planted W16 and which are "succession slots" for later.

## Don't Hand-Roll

| Problem | Don't Build | Use Instead | Why |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Companion planting compatibility | Random arrangements | The compatibility matrix in this research | Incompatible neighbors (pea+onion, strawberry+raspberry) cause real crop failure |
| Spacing calculations | Eyeball it in the garden | Pre-measured grid map with cm positions | Overcrowding is the #1 beginner mistake in raised beds; precise spacing prevents it |
| Session timing for ADHD child | Wing it on the day | Written session script with break points | ADHD executive function needs external structure; ad-hoc plans lead to meltdowns when transitions are unexpected |
| Plant markers | Buy commercial labels | Child-painted wooden markers | The craft activity builds anticipation and ownership; commercial labels miss the engagement opportunity |
| Bug hotel | Complex pre-fab kit | Simple stacked natural materials | Over-engineered kits frustrate children; loose stacking with found materials is the actual activity |

**Key insight:** The grid map and session script are the highest-value deliverables in this phase. Without the grid map, planting becomes guesswork that leads to overcrowding and companion planting violations. Without the session script, the planting day risks overwhelming the child. Both are planning documents, not physical objects -- they must be created BEFORE the planting weekend.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Companion Planting Violations
**What goes wrong:** Peas planted next to spring onions; strawberries sharing soil with raspberries. Crops underperform or contract disease.
**Why it happens:** Companion planting rules are non-obvious. People group "things that go in the ground in April" without checking compatibility.
**How to avoid:** Use the compatibility matrix above. Assign crops to beds FIRST (macro layout), then position within beds (micro layout). Cross-check every adjacency.
**Warning signs:** Any allium within 30cm of any legume. Any strawberry in the same bed as a raspberry.

### Pitfall 2: Overcrowding in a 120x60cm Bed
**What goes wrong:** Too many plant types crammed into a small bed. Plants compete for light and nutrients. Everything underperforms.
**Why it happens:** Excitement about variety. The bed is only 0.72 square meters -- that is very small.
**How to avoid:** Calculate total plant positions before committing. A 120x60cm bed fits approximately: 6-8 strawberry plants OR 3 raspberry canes with companion chives OR 15-20 radishes + 2 kale + a row of peas. Not all of the above.
**Warning signs:** Grid map with more than 5-6 distinct crop types in a single 120x60cm bed.

### Pitfall 3: Planting Day Meltdown
**What goes wrong:** The child loses interest or has an emotional crash 45 minutes in. The remaining beds don't get planted. The day feels like a failure.
**Why it happens:** ADHD executive function fatigue. Sensory overload from outdoor activity. Lack of clear structure and transitions.
**How to avoid:** Session script with: (1) his bed FIRST (most exciting, most ownership), (2) mandatory snack break after his bed, (3) shared beds as optional bonus, (4) clear end signal ("place your markers, take a photo"). Total target: under 100 minutes.
**Warning signs:** No written session plan. Planning to do "everything in one go." No snack/break scheduled.

### Pitfall 4: Forgetting Succession Sowing Slots
**What goes wrong:** Every square cm of the bed is planted on day one. Two weeks later, the first radishes are harvested but there's nowhere to sow the next batch.
**Why it happens:** Grid maps that show only the initial planting without reserving space for succession crops.
**How to avoid:** Grid maps must explicitly mark "succession slot" areas -- sections left empty on planting day that are designated for W18, W20, W22 radish/lettuce sowings.
**Warning signs:** A grid map with 100% fill on day one. No empty squares marked for future sowing.

### Pitfall 5: Lemon Balm Takes Over
**What goes wrong:** Lemon balm planted in the child's bed spreads via underground runners and crowds out strawberries within one season.
**Why it happens:** Lemon balm (Melissa officinalis) is a vigorous spreader. In a raised bed, it has nowhere to go but into neighboring plants.
**How to avoid:** Plant lemon balm in one specific corner of the bed edge. Consider planting inside a bottomless pot sunk into the soil to restrict root spread. Or plant in a separate container next to the bed.
**Warning signs:** Lemon balm positioned in the center of a bed. Multiple lemon balm plants in one bed.

## Open Questions

1. **Danish seed/plant suppliers for specific varieties**
   - What we know: The specific varieties (Ostara, Korona strawberries; Autumn Bliss raspberries; Kelvedon Wonder peas; Nero di Toscana kale) are widely available in Europe.
   - What's unclear: Which Danish nurseries or online shops stock these exact varieties at the right time.
   - Recommendation: Plan should include a shopping list task that identifies Danish suppliers (plantetorvet.dk, gartneriet.dk, bilka/bauhaus garden centers) or notes that substitutes of the same type (everbearing/June-bearing strawberry, autumn raspberry) are acceptable.

2. **Exact planting date vs. soil temperature**
   - What we know: W16 (April 13-19) is the target. Cold-hardy crops can go in when soil is workable and above 5-8C.
   - What's unclear: 2026 spring weather. Denmark can have frost into late April in cold years.
   - Recommendation: Grid maps and session script should note "weather-dependent, postpone to W17 if soil frozen or heavy frost forecast." All crops in this phase are frost-tolerant, so light frost after planting is acceptable.

3. **Lemon balm containment method**
   - What we know: It spreads aggressively. Options: (a) plant in sunken pot, (b) plant at very edge where it can be trimmed, (c) plant in separate pot next to the bed.
   - What's unclear: User preference on containment approach.
   - Recommendation: Default to planting at the outer edge of his bed with a note to trim runners monthly. If it becomes invasive, move to a separate pot in Season 2.

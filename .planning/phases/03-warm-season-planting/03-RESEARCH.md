## Summary

Phase 3 completes the garden's full planting plan by transplanting all frost-tender crops after the last frost date (May 20 for Vejle, Denmark). The phase splits into two sessions: Session 1 (W18, May 1) for direct-sowing sunflowers, nasturtiums, and bush beans, and Session 2 (W21, May 20+) for transplanting tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, basil, broccoli, and leeks, plus setting up the mint pot.

Critical research finding: **a bed space conflict exists.** The CONTEXT.md describes peppers, bush beans, and broccoli going into "the second backyard bed," but all three backyard beds are occupied -- Bed A (His Bed, strawberries/perennials), Bed B (Raspberry, perennial), and Bed C (Trellis, receiving tomatoes + cucumbers). The planner must resolve this by distributing peppers/beans/broccoli across Bed C and the terrace beds, or accept that Bed C will be densely planted. Additionally, **beans and leeks are companion-planting enemies** (alliums inhibit bean nitrogen fixation), so they must NOT share a bed -- the CONTEXT already separates them (leeks to terrace), which is correct.

The Phase 2 grid map format, session script format, and companion planting matrix all carry forward. New companion planting rules for warm-season crops (tomato+basil=good, tomato+cucumber=workable with caveats, bean+allium=bad) must be layered onto the existing bed layouts.

**Primary recommendation:** Create updated grid maps for Bed C (pea-to-cucumber handoff + tomato/basil positions), the "second backyard bed" (resolve which physical bed gets peppers/beans/broccoli -- likely Bed C overflow + terrace), and Bed D/E updates for leeks and any warm-crop additions. Write two session scripts (Session 1 direct-sow, Session 2 transplant) following the Phase 2 format. Include sunflower measurement setup and creature observation framing as lightweight add-ons, not separate deliverables.

### Pattern 1: Pea-to-Cucumber Handoff Protocol
**What:** A structured transition from spring peas to summer cucumbers on the A-frame trellis in Bed C
**When to use:** W21 (May 20+), immediately before Session 2 transplanting

**Steps:**
1. **Harvest party:** Pick ALL remaining peas together. Count them. Eat them. Celebrate.
2. **Cut peas at ground level** -- do NOT pull roots. Nitrogen-fixing nodules in the soil benefit cucumbers.
3. **Remove pea stems from trellis** -- clear the A-frame for cucumber vines.
4. **Light soil amendment:** Top up with a thin layer of compost (2-3cm) in the trellis zone.
5. **Transplant cucumber seedlings** at the trellis base, 30-45cm apart.
6. **Transition cue:** "The peas made the soil rich for the cucumbers. The trellis gets a new climber!"

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid
- **Overcrowding Bed C with 6+ warm-season crop types:** 0.72m2 is very small. Maximum 5 distinct warm-crop plants (1 cucumber, 1 tomato, 1 pepper, 2-3 basil, 1 dill) plus existing borage. If kale is still producing, it must be harvested to make room.
- **Planting bush beans next to leeks:** Alliums inhibit bean nitrogen fixation. These MUST be in separate beds. (CONTEXT already separates them -- leeks to terrace -- but the grid maps must enforce this.)
- **Letting Sungold tomato sprawl:** Sungold is indeterminate and grows 2.5-3m. Without staking and regular pruning of side shoots (suckers), it will take over the bed. Stake it on day one.
- **Planting sunflowers in the center of a bed:** Sunflowers grow 1.5-3m tall and will shade everything behind them. Place at the NORTH end of a bed so they don't block sun from shorter plants.
- **Planting nasturtiums too close to food crops:** Research suggests 60-90cm+ distance for effective trap-cropping. If nasturtiums are right next to lettuce or tomatoes, aphids hop between them. Plant nasturtiums at the bed EDGE, trailing outward.
- **Scheduling Session 2 (the big transplant day) too long without breaks:** Same ADHD pitfall as Phase 2. Mandatory snack break after his-bed portion. Total under 100 minutes.
- **Forgetting the pea harvest celebration:** Cutting peas without ceremony wastes an engagement opportunity. The harvest party IS the transition activity.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Bed Space Overcommitment
**What goes wrong:** Too many warm-season crops assigned to Bed C (0.72m2). Plants compete for light, water, and nutrients. Yields drop across all crops.
**Why it happens:** The CONTEXT names 5 warm-season crop types for backyard beds, but only one backyard bed (Bed C) is available for new planting.
**How to avoid:** Limit Bed C to 4-5 warm-season plants maximum. Overflow peppers, broccoli, or beans to the terrace beds. Use vertical growing (trellis for cucumbers, stake for tomatoes) to maximize ground space.
**Warning signs:** Grid map with more than 6 distinct crop types in Bed C. Any plant positioned less than 20cm from another.

### Pitfall 2: Sungold Tomato Takeover
**What goes wrong:** Sungold cherry tomato, being indeterminate, grows 2.5-3m and sends out endless side shoots. Without pruning, it shades out peppers, basil, and cucumbers in Bed C.
**Why it happens:** Many gardeners assume "cherry tomato" means "small plant." Sungold is vigorous and large.
**How to avoid:** Stake Sungold on transplant day. Prune side shoots (suckers) weekly -- pinch out growth in leaf axils. Train to 1-2 main stems only. Consider using Tumbling Tom as the PRIMARY variety (compact, determinate) and Sungold as a supplement in a separate pot if bed space is tight.
**Warning signs:** No tomato stake in the materials list. No suckering instructions in the session script or grid map notes.

### Pitfall 3: Bean/Leek Proximity
**What goes wrong:** Bush beans planted near leeks. Leeks (alliums) excrete compounds that inhibit the nitrogen-fixing bacteria on bean roots. Beans underperform.
**Why it happens:** Both are "cooking crops" so they seem like logical bedmates.
**How to avoid:** Different beds, no exceptions. Leeks go to Bed D (terrace), beans go to Bed E (terrace) or Bed C (backyard). Never in the same bed.
**Warning signs:** Any grid map showing beans and leeks in the same bed.

### Pitfall 4: Missing the Pea Harvest Celebration
**What goes wrong:** Father cuts peas and clears the trellis alone before Session 2. The child sees his pea plants gone and is confused or upset.
**Why it happens:** Treating pea removal as a chore rather than an event. Time pressure on transplant day.
**How to avoid:** Schedule the harvest party as the FIRST activity of Session 2, before any transplanting. Pick every last pea. Count them. Eat them. THEN clear the stems. "The peas did their job -- they made the soil rich for cucumbers."
**Warning signs:** Session script that starts with "clear the trellis" rather than "harvest party."

### Pitfall 5: Sunflower Shading
**What goes wrong:** Sunflowers planted at the south or center of a bed grow tall (1.5-3m) and block sunlight from all plants behind them.
**Why it happens:** Sunflowers are planted where there's space, without considering their height impact.
**How to avoid:** Always place sunflowers at the NORTH end/edge of a bed. In the southern hemisphere logic would be reversed, but Denmark is 55N -- sun comes from the south, so tall plants at the north end cast shadows northward into empty space, not onto other crops.
**Warning signs:** Sunflower position at south or center of any bed.

### Pitfall 6: Transplant Shock on a Hot Day
**What goes wrong:** Seedlings transplanted in full midday sun wilt and struggle. Some die.
**Why it happens:** Transplanting at noon on a sunny May day. Roots disturbed + immediate sun stress.
**How to avoid:** Transplant in the late afternoon (after 4pm) or on an overcast day. Water immediately and heavily after transplanting. The session script should note preferred weather/time conditions.
**Warning signs:** Session script with no weather/time guidance.

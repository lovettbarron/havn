### Creature-Attracting Features

The child loves creatures. These crops exist to bring life into the garden.

| Feature | What It Attracts | How to Implement | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| **Borage flowers** | Bees (heavy traffic) | Plant 3-5 plants in sunny corner | Blue flowers = bee magnets. Child can safely watch from inches away. |
| **Dill** (let some flower) | Ladybugs, hoverflies, swallowtail butterfly larvae | Direct sow May, let 2-3 plants bolt | Lacewing and hoverfly larvae eat aphids. Dill = beneficial insect nursery. |
| **Fennel flowers** | Hoverflies, parasitic wasps | Plant bronze fennel May | Beautiful bronze foliage + beneficial insects. |
| **Sunflowers** | Bees, later birds (seeds) | Direct sow May | Bee activity during flowering. Leave seed heads in autumn for finches. |
| **Nasturtium** | Aphids (trap crop), then ladybugs | Plant near but not IN veggie beds | Aphids colonize nasturtium = ladybugs arrive to eat them. Living predator-prey drama for the child. |
| **Log pile / stone pile** | Beetles, centipedes, woodlice | Stack near beds | Not a crop, but critical: flip a rock = instant creature discovery. 30-second engagement activity. |
| **Shallow water dish** | Bees, butterflies, birds | Place near flowers | Pollinators need water. Pebbles in dish for landing spots. |

## Anti-Features

Crops that seem like good ideas but are wrong for this context.

| Anti-Feature | Why It Seems Good | Why Avoid | What to Do Instead |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Corn/sweetcorn** | Kids like it; dramatic tall plant | Needs huge space, heavy feeder, wind-pollinated (needs blocks of 16+), won't reliably ripen in Danish summer in raised bed | Grow sunflowers for height drama instead |
| **Melon/watermelon** | Kids love melon | Needs sustained heat Denmark rarely provides. Long season (80-100 days of warm weather). High failure risk = devastating for ADHD engagement | Stick with cucumbers -- same family, reliable results |
| **Courgette/zucchini** | Easy, prolific | Takes enormous space in raised bed (1m+ spread). Produces SO much that novelty wears off. Overshadows smaller crops. Powdery mildew magnet in Danish humidity. | One plant max, in a separate container if at all |
| **Pumpkin/squash** | Very "garden-y" | Same space problem as courgette but worse (3m+ vines). 100+ days. Not raised-bed appropriate. | If desired, grow ONE 'Jack Be Little' mini pumpkin in its own large pot |
| **Brussels sprouts** | Grow in Denmark, family eats them | 150+ days. Tedious. Caterpillar magnet. Zero engagement value for a child. THE most boring crop. | Kale gives similar nutrition, looks cooler ("dinosaur!"), harvests faster |
| **Cauliflower** | Grows in Denmark | Fussy about temperature, bolts easily, needs blanching, pest-prone. High effort, low engagement. | Broccoli is more forgiving and produces side shoots |
| **Carrots (as main crop)** | Kids like carrots | Invisible underground = zero visual engagement for weeks. Clay/rocky soil = forked roots. Slow (70-80 days). Not exciting for ADHD. | Grow a few 'Paris Market' (round, 55 days) as a novelty, but don't make them a centerpiece |
| **Seed starting indoors** | Cheaper, educational | PROJECT.md explicitly says "direct sow or buy seedlings." Seed starting requires consistent daily attention for 6-8 weeks, equipment, space. Failure rate high for beginners. | Buy seedlings for tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers. Direct sow everything else. |
| **Exotic/unusual varieties** | Fun factor | Unfamiliar varieties have unpredictable performance. Stick with proven Scandinavian performers for year 1. | Year 2: experiment with purple carrots, white strawberries, etc. |

## Companion Planting Combinations for Raised Beds

Validated combinations. Each "bed recipe" is a compatible grouping for a standard 120x60cm or 120x80cm raised bed.

### Bed Recipe 1: "His Tomato Bed" (Full Sun, Roof Terrace)

| Position | Crop | Companion Benefit |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| Center/back | 2x Cherry tomato (Sungold + Tumbling Tom) | Main crop |
| Beside tomatoes | 2x Basil | Repels aphids/whitefly, improves tomato flavor (traditional companion) |
| Front edge | Nasturtium (trailing) | Trap crop for aphids. Cascades over edge. Edible flowers. |
| Corners | Calendula | Pest deterrent. Pollinator attractor. Edible flowers. |

**Avoid in this bed:** Dill (inhibits tomato growth), fennel (allelopathic to most vegetables), brassicas.

### Bed Recipe 2: "His Cucumber & Pepper Bed" (Full Sun)

| Position | Crop | Companion Benefit |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| Back/trellis side | 2x Cucumber (Mini Munch) on vertical support | Main crop, grows up |
| Center | 2x Sweet pepper (Lipstick + King of the North) | Main crop |
| Front | Radishes (succession sown) | Fast harvest in gaps. Loosens soil for peppers. |
| Edge | Borage | Attracts pollinators for cucumbers. Edible blue flowers. |
| Corners | Chives | Repels aphids. Edible flowers. Perennial anchor. |

**Avoid in this bed:** Sage (inhibits cucumber growth), potatoes.

### Bed Recipe 3: "Berry Bed" (Full Sun to Part Shade)

| Position | Crop | Companion Benefit |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| Back half | Raspberry (Autumn Bliss) -- 3 canes | Main crop, tall |
| Front half | Strawberry (Ostara everbearing) -- 6-8 plants | Main crop, ground level |
| Among strawberries | Borage or thyme | Borage attracts pollinators. Thyme = living mulch, aromatic. |
| Edge | Chives | Traditional strawberry companion. Repels pests. |

**Note:** This bed needs to be deeper (30cm+) and is a perennial commitment.

### Bed Recipe 4: "Family Cooking Bed" (Full Sun)

| Position | Crop | Companion Benefit |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| Back | Kale (Nero di Toscana) | Tall, dramatic |
| Center | Bush beans (Provider) | Fix nitrogen for heavy feeders |
| Center | Leeks | Repel carrot fly (if adding carrots later) |
| Front | Spring onions (White Lisbon) | Fast, compact |
| Edge | Calendula + nasturtium | Pest management, pollination, edible flowers |

## Staggered Planting Schedule for Continuous Harvest

**Design principle:** The child should be able to harvest SOMETHING every time he visits the garden from late May through October. No harvest gaps longer than 5-7 days during active season.

### Monthly Harvest Calendar

| Month | What's Ready to Pick | Engagement Activity |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **April** | Nothing yet (but planting!) | Sowing radishes, peas, lettuce. Planting strawberry/raspberry. The "building" phase. |
| **May** | First radishes (sown April 20). Pea shoots. Herb leaves. Viola flowers. | Pulling radishes = first BIG win. Tasting pea shoots. Picking viola flowers for a cake. Sow round 2 radishes. |
| **June** | Strawberries (Korona flush!). Radishes (round 2-3). Baby lettuce. Chive flowers. More herb leaves. Peas. | STRAWBERRY BONANZA. This is the engagement peak. Every visit = red berries. Count them. Weigh them. Eat them warm from the sun. |
| **July** | Strawberries (Ostara continues). Cucumbers starting. Cherry tomatoes starting mid-July. Peas. Beans. Calendula + nasturtium flowers. Garlic scapes (early July). | Tomato watching ("is it orange yet?"). First cucumber pick. Flower harvesting for weekend baking. Garlic scape twirling. |
| **JULY-AUG VACATION** | **This is the gap.** Neighbors pick strawberries, tomatoes, cucumbers. | **Vacation guide for neighbors.** Self-watering reservoirs. Thick mulch before departure. See vacation-proof section below. |
| **August** | Cherry tomatoes (PEAK). Cucumbers (peak). Peppers starting. Raspberries starting. Beans. Nasturtium/borage/calendula flowers. | Return from vacation to a LOADED garden. Tomato and cucumber bonanza. Raspberry daily picking begins. This is harvest heaven. |
| **September** | Raspberries (Autumn Bliss peak). Tomatoes (slowing). Peppers (ripe). Last cucumbers. Kale. Strawberries (Ostara autumn flush). Autumn lettuce (sown August). | Raspberry picking is the new daily ritual. Pepper harvest is colorful. Kale leaves for family cooking. |
| **October** | Raspberries (until frost). Kale (frost-hardy). Leeks. Late lettuce. Last peppers before frost. | Season wind-down. Harvest remaining. Plant garlic for next year. Put garden "to bed." |

## Vacation Survival Assessment

**Critical constraint:** 2-3 weeks mid-summer (typically late July through mid-August) with no daily care.

### Crop-by-Crop Vacation Survival (Raised Bed, No Irrigation)

| Crop | Survives 2-3 Weeks? | Mitigation | Risk Level |
|------|:-------------------:|------------|:----------:|
| Strawberry (established) | Yes, with mulch | Heavy straw mulch + one deep watering before departure. Fruit will overripen and drop -- neighbor picks. | Low |
| Raspberry (established) | Yes | Deep-rooted. Mulch base. Neighbor picks ripe fruit. | Very Low |
| Cherry Tomato | NO | Will stress, drop fruit, possibly die in raised bed. MUST have neighbor water 2x/week OR self-watering reservoir. | High |
| Cucumber | NO | Wilts within 3-4 days without water in raised bed. Same as tomato -- neighbor water or reservoir. | Very High |
| Sweet Pepper | Marginal | More drought-tolerant than tomato/cucumber. Survives with deep watering before departure + mulch + 1 neighbor watering/week. | Medium |
| Radish | N/A | Harvest before vacation. Don't have any in ground during absence. | None |
| Lettuce | N/A | Harvest before vacation. Will bolt in July heat anyway. | None |
| Bush Beans | Moderate | Tolerates some drought. Mulch. May drop flowers but survives. | Medium |
| Kale | Yes | Very drought tolerant once established. Mulch. | Very Low |
| Leeks | Yes | Very drought tolerant. Deep root system. | Very Low |
| Spring Onion | Yes | Drought tolerant. | Very Low |
| Garlic | Yes (harvested by then) | Harvest in early-mid July before vacation. | None |
| Herbs (thyme, chives) | Yes | Perennial, established root systems. | Very Low |
| Basil | NO | Wilts fast. May not survive. Accept loss or neighbor waters. | High |
| Nasturtium | Yes | Remarkably drought tolerant. Thrives on neglect. | Very Low |
| Calendula | Yes | Drought tolerant once established. | Low |
| Borage | Moderate | Self-seeds, somewhat tolerant. May look ragged but recovers. | Low-Medium |

### Vacation Strategy

1. **Self-watering reservoirs** in the tomato and cucumber beds (sub-irrigated planter inserts or DIY wicking systems). A proper SIP bed can sustain plants for 2-4 weeks.
2. **Heavy mulch** (5-8cm straw or bark) on ALL beds before departure.
3. **Neighbor guide** with photos: "Pick anything red/orange. Water these two beds twice a week. Ignore everything else."
4. **Harvest everything ripe** before leaving. Pick all tomatoes showing any color (ripen on windowsill at neighbor's house).
5. **Drip irrigation timer** is explicitly out of scope per PROJECT.md, but self-watering reservoirs are allowed. Prioritize these for the tomato and cucumber beds.

## Difficulty Tiers (From Child's Perspective)

### Tier 1: "Can't Fail" (Age 7, First Season)

These crops succeed even with irregular attention, imperfect watering, and beginner mistakes.

| Crop | Why Foolproof |
|------|--------------|
| Radish | Sow, water, pull in 3 weeks. Almost impossible to kill. |
| Nasturtium | Direct sow in poor soil. Drought-proof. Rewards neglect. |
| Strawberry (planted as pot) | Buy potted plant, put in ground, pick berries. Perennial. |
| Chives | Plant once, harvest forever. Indestructible. |
| Sunflower | Sow seed, watch it grow. Tolerates everything except deep shade. |
| Mint (in pot) | Unkillable. Pick leaves, smell them. |
| Garlic (plant in autumn) | Push clove into soil. Wait. Pull up in July. |

### Tier 2: "Needs Some Care" (Age 7 with Parent Assist)

| Crop | What Needs Attention |
|------|---------------------|
| Cherry Tomato | Regular watering. Staking. Removing suckers (parent helps). |
| Cucumber | Regular watering. Vertical training on support. Watch for mildew. |
| Sweet Pepper | Consistent watering. Slow to start (patience test). |
| Bush Bean | Sow at right time (after frost). Water during flowering. |
| Lettuce | Sow at right time. Harvest before it bolts. |
| Basil | Needs warmth. Regular pinching. Cannot dry out. |
| Calendula/Borage | Direct sow. Mostly self-managing but need initial establishment. |

### Tier 3: "Year 2 Challenge" (Not First Season)

| Crop | Why Wait |
|------|----------|
| Broccoli | Caterpillar management. Timing-sensitive. Less rewarding harvest. |
| Leek | 150-day commitment. Transplanting skill. |
| Fennel | Bolts easily. Needs consistent conditions. |
| Carrots | Invisible growth frustrates ADHD. Thin seedlings (tedious). Slow. |
| Saving seeds | Requires letting plants bolt/go to seed. Year 2 concept. |

## Feature Dependencies

```
Raised beds built + soil filled
  --> Perennials planted (strawberry, raspberry, chives, thyme) [earliest, April]
  --> Hardy direct sow (radish, pea, lettuce, spring onion, garlic already in ground) [April 15-20]
  --> Buy seedlings (tomato, pepper, cucumber) [purchase late May]
  --> Warm-season plant out (tomato, pepper, cucumber, basil, bean) [May 15-25]
  --> Edible flower direct sow (nasturtium, calendula, borage) [May 1]
  --> Succession sowing begins (radish, lettuce, beans) [ongoing every 2-3 weeks]

Self-watering reservoir installed in tomato/cucumber bed
  --> Vacation survival enabled for high-water crops

Neighbor vacation guide written
  --> Summer vacation absence covered
```

## MVP Crop List (First Season, If Budget/Space Limited)

If you can only do TWO raised beds plus a few pots:

**Must include (non-negotiable for engagement):**
1. Strawberry -- everbearing variety. Anchor crop. Continuous picking.
2. Cherry tomato -- Sungold. The daily "is it ripe?" check.
3. Radish -- succession sown. 3-week wins throughout season.
4. Nasturtium -- edible flowers for baking. Zero maintenance.
5. One herb pot (mint + chives + thyme)

**Strong second tier:**
6. Cucumber (Mini Munch) -- his favorite, snack-from-garden crop
7. Raspberry (Autumn Bliss) -- fills the Aug-Oct gap after strawberry peak
8. Sweet pepper (Lipstick) -- colorful, edible, his preference list
9. Borage -- creature attraction, edible blue stars
10. Sunflower -- height, measurement activity, bee magnet

**Defer to Year 2 or "family bed":**
- All alliums, legumes, greens, brassicas (family cooking crops)
- Carrots, broccoli, leeks
- Any Tier 3 difficulty crops

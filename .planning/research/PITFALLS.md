# Domain Pitfalls

**Domain:** Backyard learning garden for child with ADHD/Autism -- raised beds in Danish climate
**Researched:** 2026-03-26

---

## Critical Pitfalls

Mistakes that cause project abandonment, child disengagement, or major rework.

### Pitfall 1: The Waiting Game Kills ADHD Engagement

**What goes wrong:** Child plants seeds, sees dirt for 10 days, loses interest permanently. By the time seedlings emerge, gardening is "boring" and associated with disappointment. For a 7-year-old with ADHD, the dopamine window is 5-10 minutes -- if nothing visibly happened, the task fails the reward test.

**Why it happens:** Adults plan from seed packets. Seeds are cheap and feel like the "real" way to garden. But germination is invisible work -- the child sees no connection between effort and result.

**Consequences:** Child refuses to go outside to check garden. Project becomes parent-only. Core value (child agency) is dead.

**Prevention:**
- Buy seedlings/starts for the majority of crops, especially for the child's own beds. Reserve seeds only for fast-germinating, visually dramatic plants (radishes: 3-5 days, sunflowers: 5-7 days, nasturtiums: 7-10 days).
- Frame seed-starting as a specific "science experiment" activity, not the main gardening experience.
- Always have something already growing in the bed when introducing a new planting activity.

**Detection:** Child says "nothing is happening" or stops asking to go outside within the first 2 weeks.

**Phase relevance:** Crop selection and planting plan phase. Must be designed in from the start.

**Severity:** PROJECT KILLER

### Pitfall 2: Sensory Landmines in the Garden

**What goes wrong:** Child has a meltdown or develops aversion to the garden because of an unexpected sensory trigger -- slimy slug on a strawberry, wet soil texture on hands, strong herb smell (tomato leaf smell is pungent), prickly plant stems, or the visual shock of a rotting/moldy fruit.

**Why it happens:** Autism creates predictable, consistent sensory sensitivities. A child who is tactile-defensive will react to wet compost the same way every time. Once a negative sensory association forms with "the garden," it is extremely difficult to reverse. ADHD compounds this because emotional reactions are intense and immediate.

**Consequences:** Garden becomes a place of avoidance rather than agency. One bad slug encounter can set the project back weeks.

**Prevention:**
- Always have child-sized gardening gloves available (not optional -- presented as "gardener gear").
- Pre-check beds for slugs before the child's gardening session (especially mornings after rain).
- Avoid plants with strong/unpleasant odors near the child's primary interaction zone. Tomato leaves, marigold, and comfrey are common triggers.
- Remove rotting or damaged produce before the child sees it -- frame harvesting as "picking the ripe ones" not "sorting through decay."
- Have a hand-washing station or wet wipes at the garden. Sensory discomfort from dirty hands will end a session instantly.

**Detection:** Child pulls hands away, refuses to touch plants, or asks to go inside after touching something. Watch for facial grimacing when approaching specific plants.

**Phase relevance:** Bed layout phase (what goes where) and maintenance routine phase (pre-check routines).

**Severity:** PROJECT KILLER

### Pitfall 3: Vacation Gap Kills Everything Mid-Summer

**What goes wrong:** Family leaves for 2-3 weeks in July (peak Danish summer). Raised beds in full sun dry out in 2-3 days without water. Tomatoes crack, lettuce bolts, strawberries rot, cucumbers die. Family returns to devastation. Child sees dead plants he worked to grow. The emotional impact on a child with ADHD/Autism -- who formed a connection with living things -- can be severe.

**Why it happens:** Raised beds drain faster than in-ground gardens. Danish July can be warm (20-25C) with intermittent rain that is never enough. A ~70 m2 roof terrace bed with wind exposure will dry out in 24-48 hours on a warm day.

**Consequences:** Entire season's harvest lost. Child's trust in the process ("my work matters") is broken. Parents feel guilty.

**Prevention:**
- Design self-watering reservoirs into raised beds from the start (wicking bed design with water reservoir in the bottom 10-15 cm).
- Create the "neighbor vacation guide" as an actual deliverable -- simple, visual, 5 minutes of work per visit (water + pick ripe stuff).
- Thick mulch layer (5-8 cm straw or wood chips) on all beds before departure.
- Time planting schedule so peak-water-demand crops (cucumbers, tomatoes) are in self-watering beds, not standard beds.
- Roof terrace beds are highest risk -- consider drought-tolerant herbs only for terrace if no self-watering system.
- Plan harvest timing: stagger plantings so the heaviest harvest period does not coincide with vacation.

**Detection:** No vacation watering plan exists by mid-June. Neighbor has not been briefed. Beds lack mulch.

**Phase relevance:** Bed construction phase (self-watering design), planting schedule phase (timing), and documentation phase (neighbor guide).

**Severity:** PROJECT KILLER

### Pitfall 4: Danish Slug Apocalypse Destroys Seedlings Overnight

**What goes wrong:** Spanish slugs (Arion vulgaris) eat every seedling in a single night. Denmark has been dealing with a Spanish slug invasion since the 1990s. These slugs are large, prolific (hundreds of eggs per season), and have almost no natural predators. A single rainy night in June can result in a bed of bare stems where lettuce and strawberry plants used to be.

**Why it happens:** Raised beds with fresh compost and young plants are a slug buffet. Danish maritime climate (cool, damp nights) is perfect slug weather. Spanish slugs are more aggressive feeders than native slugs.

**Consequences:** Child's plants destroyed. Research from Aarhus University confirms most "home remedies" (beer traps, eggshells, coffee grounds) are ineffective against Spanish slugs. Repeated losses create a "why bother" attitude.

**Prevention:**
- Copper tape around the top edge of all raised beds. Not 100% effective (research says 80-90% deterrence) but significantly reduces slug access. Corten steel beds pair well with copper tape aesthetically.
- Bed height matters: 40+ cm beds with smooth metal sides are substantially harder for slugs to climb.
- Evening slug patrol with the child (make it a game: "slug safari with a flashlight"). Manual collection is the only scientifically validated control method per Aarhus University research.
- Avoid ground-level mulch paths between beds -- slugs hide in mulch during the day.
- Plant slug-resistant varieties where possible: herbs (rosemary, thyme, sage), strawberries (less preferred than lettuce), avoid lettuce in ground-level beds entirely.

**Detection:** Shiny slime trails on bed edges in the morning. Irregular holes in leaves. Seedlings disappearing overnight.

**Phase relevance:** Bed construction phase (height, copper tape) and crop selection phase (slug-resistant varieties).

**Severity:** HIGH -- will not kill project if caught early, but repeated losses will erode motivation.

### Pitfall 5: Roof Terrace Structural Overload

**What goes wrong:** Wet soil weighs approximately 1,200 kg per cubic meter. A 1m x 0.5m x 0.3m raised bed contains 0.15 m3 of soil = ~180 kg when saturated. Place two of those on a roof terrace built decades ago above a carport, and you risk structural damage, cracking, or (worst case) collapse.

**Why it happens:** People visualize soil as "light, fluffy garden stuff." Wet soil is essentially liquid mud -- dense and heavy. A older carport roof was not designed for distributed static loads of this magnitude.

**Consequences:** Structural damage to the carport. Water damage from drainage. Expensive repairs. Safety hazard.

**Prevention:**
- Get the roof's load-bearing capacity assessed before placing any beds. This is non-negotiable for an olderstructure.
- Use lightweight soil mixes: perlite, vermiculite, coconut coir instead of standard topsoil. Can reduce weight by 30-50%.
- Shallower beds on terrace (maximum 20 cm depth). Herbs and strawberries do fine in 15-20 cm.
- Place beds over structural supports (walls/beams below), never in the center of an unsupported span.
- Use fabric or lightweight composite planters, not corten steel, on the terrace.
- Ensure drainage does not pool on the terrace surface -- standing water adds dead weight and damages roofing membrane.

**Detection:** Any visible sagging, cracking, or water pooling on the terrace surface. Difficulty opening doors/windows near the terrace (indicates structural movement).

**Phase relevance:** Bed placement and construction phase. Must be resolved before any beds go on the terrace.

**Severity:** SAFETY CRITICAL -- not a gardening problem, a structural safety problem.

## Moderate Pitfalls

### Pitfall 6: Overcomplicating the Child's Routine

**What goes wrong:** Parent creates a 15-step daily garden routine. Child with ADHD cannot hold a multi-step sequence in working memory. Routine becomes a source of conflict ("you forgot to...") instead of agency.

**Prevention:**
- Maximum 3 steps per garden visit: check (look at plants), do (one action: water OR pick OR weed), done.
- Visual checklist with pictures, laminated, hanging at bed height. Not a written list -- a picture sequence.
- Rotate the "do" task daily so it stays novel. ADHD brains need variety even within routine.
- Never correct during the session. If he waters the wrong bed, that bed got extra water. Agency means accepting imperfect execution.

**Detection:** Child starts resisting or saying "I don't want to" when asked to go to the garden.

**Phase relevance:** Maintenance routine phase.

**Severity:** MODERATE -- erodes engagement slowly rather than killing it instantly.

### Pitfall 7: Wrong Crops for Danish Outdoor Growing

**What goes wrong:** Peppers, tomatoes, and cucumbers are on the child's favorites list, but these are all warm-season crops that struggle outdoors in Denmark without protection. Outdoor tomatoes in Denmark face late blight (Phytophthora infestans) in cool, wet August weather. Peppers may not ripen at all in a cool summer. Cucumbers need consistent warmth.

**Prevention:**
- Tomatoes: choose cold-tolerant, early-ripening varieties (e.g., 'Sungold', 'Stupice', 'Sub Arctic Plenty'). Plan for some form of rain cover (simple overhead plastic sheet) to prevent blight splashing.
- Peppers: only attempt on the roof terrace (warmest microclimate, most sun). Use black fabric pots that absorb heat. Choose fast-maturing varieties. Accept they may not produce in a cool year -- manage child's expectations.
- Cucumbers: outdoor varieties exist for Scandinavia (e.g., 'Marketmore', 'Sonja'). Need wind protection.
- Strawberries: the safest crowd-pleaser. Everbearing varieties produce from June through September. Plant these as the primary "his bed" crop.
- Have "guaranteed win" crops (radishes, strawberries, herbs, nasturtiums) alongside "stretch" crops (tomatoes, peppers). If stretch crops fail, wins still exist.

**Detection:** Mid-July and tomato plants look yellow/spotted, peppers have flowers but no fruit, cucumbers are stunted.

**Phase relevance:** Crop selection phase.

**Severity:** MODERATE -- affects harvest but not project viability if guaranteed-win crops are in place.

### Pitfall 8: Soil Fill Cost Shock

**What goes wrong:** Budget is 2000-5000 DKK. Corten steel beds alone can consume the entire budget. Then soil fill arrives as a massive unexpected cost. Quality raised bed soil mix in Denmark costs 500-1000 DKK per cubic meter. Two or three beds can easily need 0.5-1.0 m3 of soil. Suddenly the project costs 8000-10000 DKK.

**Prevention:**
- Budget soil as a line item from day one. It often costs more than the frame.
- Use the "lasagna" / hugelkultur layering method: bottom layer of branches/cardboard (free), middle layer of leaves/grass clippings (free), top 15-20 cm of quality compost mix (purchased). Reduces purchased soil volume by 40-60%.
- Get bulk delivery, not bags. Bagged soil from garden centers costs 2-4x more per liter than bulk delivery.
- If corten steel pushes past budget, use untreated larch or Douglas fir boards for ground-level beds and reserve corten for the visible terrace beds only.
- Seeds and seedlings: budget 300-500 DKK. Tools: budget 200-400 DKK. Copper tape, mulch, amendments: 200-300 DKK. These add up.

**Detection:** No itemized budget exists. Only the bed frames are priced.

**Phase relevance:** Materials and shopping list phase.

**Severity:** MODERATE -- will not kill the project but may force compromises or delays.

### Pitfall 9: Zigbee Sensors Fail Outdoors

**What goes wrong:** Indoor-rated Zigbee soil moisture sensors (Xiaomi/Aqara flower sensors are the most common) are placed in raised beds. They are not waterproof (no IP rating). First heavy rain kills them. Alternatively, Zigbee signal cannot reach from garden to house -- outdoor range is dramatically shorter than indoor range due to lack of reflecting surfaces. Sensors report intermittently or drop off the network.

**Prevention:**
- Only use IP65+ rated sensors for outdoor deployment. The ThirdReality Zigbee soil moisture sensor is purpose-built for this. The Spruce by Plaid Systems is another option.
- Zigbee outdoor range: plan for maximum 10-15 meters line-of-sight. Place a Zigbee router/repeater (mains-powered outdoor smart plug in a weatherproof enclosure) between house and garden.
- Soil moisture readings vary wildly between sensor brands -- calibrate against manual checks (finger test) for the first 2 weeks before trusting automation.
- Start with manual watering assessment, add sensors in a later phase. The data format (JSON schema) should be ready, but physical sensors are not critical for season one.
- Battery life: outdoor temperature extremes drain batteries faster. Plan for battery replacement every 3-6 months, not the 12 months the spec sheet claims.

**Detection:** Sensors showing "unavailable" in Home Assistant. Moisture readings that do not change when you water the bed.

**Phase relevance:** Home Assistant / IoT phase. Explicitly a later phase -- do not let sensor setup delay planting.

**Severity:** MODERATE -- sensors are nice-to-have for season one, not critical path.

### Pitfall 10: Wind Exposure on Roof Terrace

**What goes wrong:** The ~70 m2 roof terrace is elevated and exposed. Wind desiccates plants (dries out leaves faster than roots can supply water), breaks stems, blows over lightweight pots, and makes the terrace unpleasant for a child to work in. Vejle is in eastern Jutland with significant wind exposure.

**Prevention:**
- Wind-tolerant plants only on terrace: herbs (thyme, rosemary, sage, chives), strawberries, compact cherry tomatoes in heavy pots.
- No tall/leggy plants on terrace (no staked tomatoes, no sunflowers, no climbing beans).
- Position beds against the wall/railing that faces away from prevailing wind (westerly in Denmark).
- Consider a simple windbreak: a trellis with climbing nasturtiums serves dual purpose.
- Water terrace beds more frequently -- wind increases evaporation significantly.

**Detection:** Plants on terrace are wilting even when soil is moist (wind damage, not drought). Broken stems.

**Phase relevance:** Bed placement phase and crop assignment phase.

**Severity:** MODERATE

## Minor Pitfalls

### Pitfall 11: Companion Planting Overcrowding

**What goes wrong:** Trying to fit too many companion planting combinations into a small raised bed. A 1m x 1m bed has about 9-16 planting squares (square-foot method). Fitting tomatoes + basil + marigolds + onions + lettuce means everything is competing for light and root space. In a small bed, every plant shades its neighbor.

**Prevention:**
- Keep companion groupings simple: maximum 2-3 species per small bed.
- For the child's bed: monoculture is fine. A bed of just strawberries is easier to manage and more rewarding than a complex polyculture.
- Reserve companion planting complexity for family beds where the parent manages it.
- Remember: alliums (onions, garlic) inhibit nitrogen-fixing bacteria and should not be planted near beans or peas.

**Phase relevance:** Planting plan phase.

**Severity:** LOW -- results in reduced yields, not failure.

---

### Pitfall 12: Planting Too Early After Last Frost

**What goes wrong:** Excitement leads to planting warm-season crops in early April. A late frost (common in Denmark through mid-April, occasionally into early May) kills tender seedlings. Child's plants die through no fault of his care routine.

**Prevention:**
- Hard rule: no tender plants outdoors before May 1st in Vejle. Even if the weather looks warm in April.
- Use April for soil preparation, bed setup, and planting cold-hardy crops only (radishes, peas, spinach, lettuce).
- Have fleece/frost cloth ready for unexpected late frosts in May.
- Frame it positively: "April is when we get the beds ready for the big planting day in May."

**Phase relevance:** Planting schedule phase.

**Severity:** LOW -- one-time setback, easily recovered if spare seedlings are available.

### Pitfall 13: Drainage Onto Terrace Surface

**What goes wrong:** Raised beds on the terrace drain onto the surface. Constant moisture damages the roofing membrane, creates algae/moss growth (slippery -- safety hazard for a 7-year-old), and stains the surface. In winter, trapped water freezes and causes frost damage.

**Prevention:**
- All terrace beds must have saucers/trays underneath to catch drainage.
- Elevate beds on feet/risers (2-3 cm) for airflow underneath.
- Position drainage toward terrace drain, not toward seating area.
- Check that existing terrace drainage can handle the additional water volume.

**Phase relevance:** Bed construction phase (terrace-specific).

**Severity:** LOW in the short term, but cumulative damage becomes expensive.

---

### Pitfall 14: Ignoring the "Creatures" Engagement Hook

**What goes wrong:** The project focuses entirely on plants but the child's engagement pattern notes he is drawn to creatures/nature. Missing this hook means missing the most powerful motivator. Gardens are full of insects, worms, ladybugs, bees -- but only if you design for them.

**Prevention:**
- Include at least one pollinator-attracting plant per bed (nasturtiums, borage, lavender).
- Frame worm sightings as positive ("the worms are helping your plants!").
- Consider a small "bug hotel" near the beds -- a project the child can build.
- Keep a "creature log" -- counting creatures is another engagement hook (systems/counting interest).

**Phase relevance:** Crop selection and garden design phase.

**Severity:** LOW -- missed opportunity rather than failure, but easy win to include.

## Phase-Specific Warnings

| Phase Topic | Likely Pitfall | Mitigation |
|-------------|---------------|------------|
| Bed placement | Terrace structural overload | Assess load capacity before any terrace beds |
| Bed placement | Inadequate sun mapping | Map shadow patterns from mature trees on east side across full day before deciding ground-level bed positions |
| Bed construction | Soil cost blows budget | Budget soil as primary line item, use lasagna layering |
| Bed construction | No slug defense built in | Copper tape and 40+ cm smooth-sided beds from day one |
| Bed construction (terrace) | Weight, drainage, wind | Lightweight soil, shallow beds, saucers, wind-tolerant plants only |
| Crop selection | All crops are "stretch" difficulty | Ensure 60%+ of plantings are guaranteed-win crops |
| Crop selection | Ignoring sensory triggers | No pungent-leaf plants in child's primary interaction zone |
| Planting schedule | Planting too early | Hard May 1st rule for tender crops |
| Planting schedule | Peak harvest = vacation | Stagger plantings so harvest window does not peak during July departure |
| Maintenance routine | Too many steps | 3-step max: check, do, done |
| Maintenance routine | Correcting the child | Let imperfect execution stand -- agency over optimization |
| Vacation planning | No watering plan | Self-watering reservoirs + neighbor guide + mulch -- all before June |
| IoT / sensors | Indoor sensors used outdoors | IP65+ only, Zigbee repeater in garden area, defer to phase 2 |
| Companion planting | Overcrowding small beds | Max 2-3 species, monoculture fine for child's beds |
